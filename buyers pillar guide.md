## The 2026 Real Estate Landscape: What Buyers Need to Know

The housing market and the rules around buying have changed significantly. Here's what's in effect now.

### Commission Transparency

Since August 2024, buyer agent fees must be negotiated and documented in writing before you tour any home. Sellers can still offer to cover your agent's fee — they just can't post it on the MLS. Every cost must be spelled out in your buyer-broker agreement before you start shopping.

### Buyer-Broker Agreements Are Now Standard

In New York and most of the country, you'll sign a written agreement with your agent before touring homes. That agreement must state exactly what your agent will be paid — a flat dollar amount, a percentage, or an hourly rate — and must confirm that the fee is negotiable.

### New York Requires an Attorney — Not Just at Closing

New York is an attorney state. Both buyer and seller are required by law to have their own real estate attorney. Critically, only an attorney can draft or review a sales contract in New York — your agent cannot do this legally. Budget for attorney fees as a standard line item, not an optional add-on.

### Digital Closings Are Growing — But NY Is Still In-Person

About 90% of lenders now offer some form of digital closing, and hybrid closings (where many documents are e-signed in advance) are increasingly common. However, New York's attorney-closing requirement means you'll still typically have a formal closing with all parties present. Expect some paperwork to be digital, but plan for an in-person event.

### Mortgage Rates: Elevated but Stabilizing

As of mid-2026, the 30-year fixed-rate mortgage is averaging around 6.4% — down from highs above 7% in 2023, but well above the sub-3% era of 2021. Most forecasters expect rates to stay above 6% through the rest of 2026. Rate lock strategy and timing still matter.

## Step 1: Determine Your Budget

Buying a home starts long before you walk into an open house. It begins with knowing exactly what you can afford. Understanding your finances and setting a realistic budget is the first move — and the one that protects you from stress at every step that follows.

### Assess Your Financial Health

Think of your finances like the foundation of a house. If the base is unstable, the whole structure is at risk. Start by looking closely at:

- **Income:** Your monthly take-home pay
- **Savings:** Available funds for the down payment, closing costs, and an emergency reserve
- **Monthly Expenses:** Bills, debt payments, groceries, childcare, and discretionary spending

A healthy budget doesn't stretch you to your breaking point. Most financial guidelines suggest your monthly housing costs — mortgage, taxes, and insurance — should not exceed 28% of your gross monthly income. Total debt payments, including car loans and credit cards, should stay under 36%.

Owning a home also comes with ongoing costs: maintenance, repairs, and utilities. Having at least 3 to 6 months of living expenses saved as an emergency fund gives you a real buffer after closing.

**What does a larger down payment do?**
A larger down payment reduces your loan amount and your monthly payment. - **Property Taxes and Insurance:** Vary by area; often built into your monthly payment through escrow
- **HOA Fees:** If the home is in a managed community
- **Maintenance:** Set aside 1–3% of the home's value per year for repairs and upkeep

### Why Budgeting First Matters

Getting financially clear before you start house hunting saves you time, helps your agent focus on the right listings, and keeps emotions in check. It also sets you up for a smoother pre-approval process in Step 2.

Think of it as mapping your route before you start driving. You'll know your limits and can avoid costly detours.

## Step 2: Get Pre-Approved for a Mortgage

Before you start touring homes or making offers, getting pre-approved for a mortgage sets the stage for a confident buying process. This step explains what pre-approval is, why it matters, what documents you'll need, and how to choose a lender.

### What Does "Pre-Approval" Mean?

Pre-approval is an official letter from a lender stating how much you could borrow, based on a review of your finances. With a pre-approval letter in hand, agents and sellers see you as a serious buyer. In competitive markets, it can be the difference between getting an offer accepted and losing a home to another buyer.

Pre-approval is not a guarantee of final loan approval — but it moves you to the front of the line and gives you a realistic budget ceiling.

### Why Pre-Approval Matters

- **Shows sellers you're ready to buy:** Sellers often prefer buyers with pre-approval, especially in fast-moving markets
- **Narrows your search:** You know exactly how much you can spend
- **Speeds up the process:** When you find the right home, you can move quickly
- **Surfaces problems early:** If your credit or income needs work, you'll know before you get too attached to a property

### What Documents You'll Need

Be organized. The more complete your paperwork, the faster the process:

- Proof of income: recent pay stubs, W-2 forms, or tax returns (last 2 years)
- Bank statements showing assets and savings
- Proof of employment: employer contact information, sometimes a verification letter
- Proof of identity: driver's license or state ID
- Debt information: statements for student loans, auto loans, credit cards
- Other assets: retirement or investment account summaries

### How to Choose a Mortgage Lender

Not all lenders are alike. Your choice affects your rate, fees, and how smoothly the process runs:

- **Compare interest rates:** Even a small difference can add up to thousands of dollars over the life of a loan
- **Check lender reviews:** Look for transparency, clear communication, and reliable timelines
- **Ask about closing timelines:** In a competitive market, a slow lender can cost you a deal
- **Consider loan options:** Banks, credit unions, and online lenders all offer different programs — FHA, VA, conventional fixed-rate, and adjustable-rate
- **Factor in fees:** Ask for a full breakdown of total costs, including origination fees, appraisal fees, and any miscellaneous charges

A lender who explains everything in plain language and responds quickly is worth prioritizing over a marginally lower rate with poor service.

### Key Takeaways

- Get pre-approved before you shop. It's your buying credential.
- Gather paperwork early. Organization makes the process faster and less stressful.
- Shop lenders as carefully as you shop homes. Prioritize service, transparency, and fair rates.
- Pre-approval is not final approval, but it puts you in a stronger position with sellers.

## Step 4: Start House Hunting

With your budget set and your agent in place, it's time to start looking at homes. This step is about finding properties that fit your needs and learning what to pay attention to when you walk through them.

### Creating Your Wishlist

Before you start touring, list your must-haves versus nice-to-haves. If you're buying with a partner, align on priorities before the search starts — disagreements are harder to resolve once you're emotionally attached to a specific property.

Consider:

- Number of bedrooms and bathrooms
- Type of home (single-family, condo, townhouse)
- Yard or outdoor space
- Proximity to work, schools, or amenities
- Neighborhood character and safety
- Accessibility features
- Long-term growth potential

A clear wishlist helps your agent filter listings efficiently and reduces wasted visits.

### How Your Agent Helps With House Hunting

Your agent handles the logistics so you can focus on evaluating homes. They will:

- **Schedule tours and open house visits:** They coordinate with sellers and listing agents and arrange private showings on your behalf
- **Share new listings:** Agents often have early notice of homes coming to market before they appear publicly online
- **Provide local insights:** Market knowledge, potential issues with specific properties, and honest comparisons
- **Answer your questions:** From neighborhood trends to property condition, your agent is your first resource

### What to Look for During Showings

Photos don't tell the full story. In person, pay attention to:

- **General condition:** Walls, floors, ceilings, and windows — look for cracks, water stains, or signs of mold
- **Layout and flow:** Walk through rooms to see if the space works for how you actually live
- **Natural light and ventilation:** How much sunlight does the home get? Does the air feel fresh?
- **Noise levels:** Street noise, neighbors, and other distractions are easier to evaluate in person
- **Storage space:** Closets, cabinets, and garage room
- **Appliances and systems:** Ask about the age and condition of HVAC, water heater, and electrical panel
- **Safety and maintenance:** Working smoke detectors, secure locks, signs of routine upkeep
- **Neighborhood and street:** Visit at different times of day to gauge traffic, parking, and atmosphere
- **Anything that feels off:** Trust your instincts. Your agent can recommend inspections or specialists for further evaluation

### Tips to Make the Most of Each Visit

- Take notes and photos for comparison — don't rely on memory
- Bring your wishlist and grade each home against it
- Don't be afraid to revisit a home before making an offer
- Think about your long-term plans, not just what you want right now

### Summary

House hunting is part discovery, part decision-making. Your agent handles scheduling and coordination so you can stay focused on evaluating properties. Clear communication with your agent and a consistent set of criteria will help you identify the right home without wasting time on the wrong ones.

## Step 5: Make an Offer

You've found the right home. Now it's time to make a formal offer. This step can feel high-stakes, but with the right agent and the right preparation, it's a manageable and well-defined process.

### Understanding the Offer Process

Your agent will guide you in drafting a written offer. The document outlines your proposed purchase price, desired closing date, and any conditions attached to the sale. In competitive markets, sellers may receive multiple offers. Your agent will help you strategize how to make your offer strong without overextending your budget.

A typical offer includes:

- **Purchase price:** What you're willing to pay
- **Earnest money deposit:** A good-faith payment held in escrow
- **Proposed closing date**
- **Contingencies:** Conditions that must be met for the sale to proceed

### Common Contingencies

- **Financing contingency:** Lets you exit if your mortgage isn't approved
- **Inspection contingency:** Allows you to renegotiate or withdraw if major problems surface during inspection
- **Appraisal contingency:** Protects you if the home appraises below your offer price
- **Sale of current home contingency:** Gives you an exit option if your existing home doesn't sell

Since August 2024, all fee and commission arrangements must be clearly spelled out in your buyer-broker agreement — signed before you tour any home. Your purchase agreement will also document these terms.

### Negotiation

Don't expect an immediate "yes." Sellers may accept, reject, or counter with different terms — a higher price, adjusted timeline, or removal of contingencies.

Your agent will help you respond professionally and negotiate on price, closing costs, repair credits, and move-in date. The goal is an outcome that works for both sides.

### Once Your Offer Is Accepted

When both parties sign the purchase agreement — often using a secure e-signature platform — the transaction enters the due diligence period. This is when inspections, appraisals, and title work happen. It's also when an escrow company steps in as a neutral third party to hold funds and protect both buyer and seller until closing.

In New York, your real estate attorney will be actively involved from this point forward. Only your attorney can draft or review the contract — this is required by law.

Your agent will coordinate the next steps and keep you updated throughout.

## Step 6: Offer Accepted — Due Diligence Period

Your offer is accepted. Now begins the due diligence period — a protected window in which you investigate every aspect of the home before fully committing to the purchase. Think of it as your chance to look under the hood before you sign anything final.

### What Is the Due Diligence Period?

The due diligence period is a negotiated timeframe — typically 14 to 30 days — written into your purchase agreement. During this phase, you investigate the property in detail, check for issues, and retain the right to back out if serious problems surface. Your real estate agent and attorney will keep you organized and on deadline.

### What Happens During Due Diligence

**Home Inspection**
A licensed inspector examines the property's structure, roof, foundation, electrical and plumbing systems, appliances, and more. You'll receive a detailed written report. If major issues surface, your agent can help you negotiate repairs or credits with the seller. An inspection is not always legally required, but it is almost always worth doing.

**Title Search**
The title company reviews public records to verify legal ownership and check for liens, claims, or disputes on the property. A clean title is required for a smooth closing. In New York, your attorney oversees this process.

**Appraisal**
Your lender will order an independent appraisal to confirm the property's market value matches your offer. This protects both you and the lender from overpaying. If the appraisal comes in below your offer price, your appraisal contingency gives you options.

**Survey and Boundaries**
Some buyers order a property survey to confirm lot lines, easements, and that there are no encroachments or boundary disputes.

**Review Disclosures**
The seller is legally required to disclose known defects, pests, environmental hazards, and previous repairs. Read these carefully and ask your attorney about anything unclear.

**HOA Documents (if applicable)**
Review any homeowners' association rules, bylaws, and fee schedules so you understand community obligations before you're bound by them.

**Utilities, Taxes, and Insurance**
Confirm utility connections, property tax amounts, and insurance costs. Check whether the home is in a flood or hazard zone — this affects your insurance requirements.

### Pro Tips

- Use a checklist. Ask your agent or attorney for one that covers every required step.
- Be prompt. Schedule inspections and return paperwork quickly. Delays can put your closing date at risk.
- Know your rights. If serious problems surface, your contingencies may allow you to renegotiate or exit with minimal financial penalty.

### What Happens Next

When due diligence is complete and you're satisfied with the results, you'll formally release your contingencies and move toward closing. If you discover issues, you'll work with your agent and attorney to decide whether to request repairs, renegotiate price, or — in rare cases — withdraw your offer.

## Step 7: Secure Financing

Your offer has been accepted and due diligence is underway or wrapping up. Now it's time to finalize your mortgage so the funds are ready for closing day.

Your lender reviewed your information for pre-approval. Now they'll do a deeper review — verifying everything and confirming the property meets lending standards.

### The Mortgage Finalization Process

**1. Submit a Complete Loan Application**

Even if you completed parts of this for pre-approval, your lender will now need the full package:

- Updated pay stubs, W-2s, or tax returns
- Current bank statements
- Details about any debts or additional assets
- The signed purchase agreement and property details

**2. Loan Estimate Review**

Within three business days, your lender will provide a Loan Estimate — a document outlining your loan terms, interest rate, monthly payment, and estimated closing costs. Review it carefully with your attorney to confirm it matches your budget and what you agreed upon.

**3. Additional Documentation Requests**

Loan processors may ask for extra documentation: employment verification, updated bank balances, or an explanation of any changes since pre-approval. Respond quickly to avoid delays.

**4. Appraisal, Title Work, and Insurance**

These often happen concurrently with the tail end of due diligence:

- The title search confirms no liens or claims on the home
- Homeowner's insurance must be secured before final loan approval

**5. Underwriting**

Underwriting is your loan's final review. The underwriter examines every document, the property's appraised value, and your financial profile to confirm you meet all lending requirements. If conditions are issued — requests for additional documentation or clarification — handle them promptly.

**6. Final Loan Approval**

Once underwriting is complete and all conditions are satisfied, your lender issues a "Clear to Close." At least three business days before closing, you'll receive a Closing Disclosure — a final, itemized breakdown of loan terms, closing costs, and exactly how much you'll need to bring to the table.

### 2026 Considerations

- **Commission rules:** If you've agreed to contribute toward your agent's fee, confirm this with your lender early. Some loan programs limit the credits or contributions allowed.
- **Digital closings:** Many lenders now send the majority of closing documents for e-signature before closing day, reducing paperwork at the table. In New York, plan for an in-person closing with your attorney present — this is required.
- **Rate locks:** With rates around 6.4% for a 30-year fixed (mid-2026), ask your lender about locking your rate. A rate lock protects you from increases between now and closing day.

### Key Takeaways

- Be prompt and responsive. Mortgage finalization has many moving parts and tight timelines.
- Use your attorney as a resource when reviewing loan documents, especially in New York.
- Don't make major financial moves during this period — buying a car, opening new credit, or changing jobs can affect your loan approval.
- A "Clear to Close" puts you one step from the closing table.

## Step 8: Review and Sign Closing Documents

After securing financing, the next step is reviewing and signing the closing documents. This is where everything is made official — your mortgage terms are locked in and ownership transfers to you.

### What "Closing" Means

Closing — also called settlement — is the formal meeting where you, the seller, your agents, your lender, and your attorney finalize the transaction. You'll sign legal documents transferring ownership, confirm payment of funds, and become the legal owner of the property.

In New York, your real estate attorney is present at closing and required by law. They will review documents on your behalf, explain what you're signing, and ensure the transaction is properly executed.

### Key Documents You'll Review and Sign

- **Closing Disclosure (CD):** Provided at least three business days before closing. Summarizes final loan terms, interest rate, monthly payment, closing costs, and the total amount you need to bring to closing. Review it carefully for accuracy before closing day.
- **Loan Agreement / Promissory Note:** Your legal promise to repay the mortgage. It includes the loan amount, interest rate, payment schedule, and penalties for late payment.
- **Deed of Trust or Mortgage:** Secures the lender's interest in your home and outlines the conditions under which foreclosure may occur.
- **Mortgage Disclosure Statement:** Shows the full cost of the loan, including finance charges and annual percentage rate (APR).
- **Title Insurance Policy:** Protects you and your lender against disputes over ownership or prior claims on the property.
- **Bill of Sale:** Transfers ownership of any personal property included in the sale — appliances, fixtures, and similar items.
- **Property Inspection Reports and Disclosures:** Final acknowledgment of known issues or conditions.
- **Affidavits and Declarations:** Legal statements confirming facts about the property or your identity.
- **Escrow Statement:** Details funds held in escrow for taxes, insurance, and other ongoing expenses.

### Tips for a Smooth Signing

- **Read in advance:** Review your Closing Disclosure and loan documents before closing day. Ask your attorney about anything unclear.
- **Bring identification:** A government-issued photo ID is required.
- **Ask questions:** Don't hesitate to pause and get clarification on any clause or figure.
- **Check all numbers:** Confirm that loan amounts, rates, and fees match what you agreed upon.
- **Understand what you're signing:** Your attorney is there to help you — use them.

### Closing in New York

New York closings require attorney presence for both buyer and seller. Even if you've e-signed some documents in advance, plan for an in-person event with your attorney, the seller's attorney, and typically a representative from the title company. Remote-only closings are not standard in New York.

### After You Sign

Once all documents are signed and funds are confirmed:

- The closing agent records the deed and mortgage with the county
- Funds are distributed to the seller, agents, and other parties
- You receive copies of all finalized documents
- You are the homeowner

## Step 9: Closing the Sale

This is the final step before the home is yours. Every piece of the process — budgeting, pre-approval, due diligence, financing — comes together at closing. By the end, ownership officially transfers from the seller to you.

### What Happens at Closing

Closing is coordinated by a closing agent, escrow company, or real estate attorney. In New York, an attorney is required. Here's what to expect:

- **Final document review and signing:** You'll sign the last of the legal documents, including mortgage agreements and the deed. Your attorney reviews each one with you.
- **Fund transfer:** You'll pay your down payment and closing costs — typically by cashier's check or wire transfer. Confirm the method with your closing agent in advance. Your lender sends the loan funds directly to the closing agent, who distributes money to the seller, agents, and any other parties owed in the transaction.
- **Recording the sale:** The closing agent files the new deed with your local county recorder's office. This public record officially establishes you as the legal owner.
- **Exchange of keys:** Once funds are received and confirmed, you get the keys.

### Pro Tips for Closing Day

- **Do a final walk-through first:** Typically scheduled within 24 hours before closing. Confirm the property is in the agreed condition and that any negotiated repairs have been completed.
- **Watch for wire fraud:** If you're wiring funds, verify payment instructions directly with your closing agent using a known phone number — not contact information from a recent email. Fraud targeting real estate closings is a real and growing problem.
- **Bring your ID:** A government-issued photo ID is required.
- **Take your time:** You are under no obligation to rush. Understand each document before you sign it. Your attorney is there for exactly this reason.

### 2026 Closing Trends

- **Hybrid e-signing:** Most lenders now send the majority of closing documents for e-signature before closing day, reducing the amount of paperwork at the table. This is now standard practice.
- **Digital closings:** Many buyers can sign documents electronically and notarize remotely. In New York, plan for an in-person attorney-attended closing regardless of what can be handled digitally in advance.

### After Closing

A few immediate steps to take once you have the keys:

- Store all signed documents in a safe location — both digital and physical copies
- Change the locks and update any smart home device credentials
- Contact utility companies to transfer all services into your name

## Step 10: Post-Closing Steps

The paperwork is signed, the funds are transferred, and the deed is recorded in your name. The buying process is complete. But a few important steps remain to protect your property and set yourself up for what comes next.

### 1. Move In

- Coordinate your move around your possession date as stated in the purchase agreement
- If buying from an occupied home, confirm keys are exchanged only after the seller has vacated, unless a leaseback arrangement was agreed upon

### 2. Change the Locks and Update Security

Change all exterior locks and reprogram or replace smart locks and garage door codes as soon as possible. You have no way of knowing how many copies of the old keys exist. If the home has a smart security system, update all passwords and connect it to your account.

### 3. Transfer Utilities

Contact local utility companies to set up accounts in your name:

- Electricity
- Water and sewer
- Gas
- Internet and cable
- Trash and recycling

Schedule transfers for your move-in date to avoid any interruption of service.

### 4. Review Your Homeowner's Insurance

You provided proof of insurance for closing, but take a moment to confirm your coverage is adequate:

- Policy limits should be sufficient to cover rebuilding costs if necessary
- Coverage should include personal belongings, liability protection, and natural disasters common to your area (flood, wind, etc.)

### 5. Organize and Safeguard Your Documents

Keep copies of:

- Closing Disclosure
- Deed
- Mortgage Note
- Title Insurance Policy
- Inspection Reports
- Warranties for appliances or major systems

Store both physical and digital backups in secure locations. You'll need these for taxes, refinancing, or selling in the future.

### 6. Plan for Ongoing Costs

Owning a home means ongoing expenses:

- Property taxes
- HOA dues (if applicable)
- Maintenance and repairs: set aside 1–3% of the home's value per year
- Landscaping, pest control, and seasonal upkeep

### 7. Update Your Address

Update your mailing address with:

- USPS (to forward mail)
- Banks and credit cards
- Employer and payroll
- Insurance providers
- DMV (driver's license and vehicle registration)
- Subscriptions and online accounts

### 8. Get to Know Your Neighborhood

Meet your neighbors, find local amenities, learn trash pickup schedules, and locate nearby emergency services. The community around your home matters as much as the home itself.

### Key Takeaways

- Post-closing is about protection, preparation, and settling in
- Secure the property and organize your finances right away
- Keep all important documents safe — you'll need them for taxes, refinancing, or a future sale

You've completed the home buying journey.

## FAQ

**What are closing costs and how much should I expect?**
Closing costs are fees paid at the end of the transaction — lender charges, title fees, taxes, attorney fees, and more. In New York, expect to budget 2–5% of the purchase price. On a $300,000 home, that's $6,000 to $15,000. Use Closing Map's closing cost calculator for an itemized estimate based on your specific situation.

**How do I figure out what home I can afford?**
Start with your monthly take-home income, subtract your regular bills and debt payments, and see what's left for housing. The standard guideline is to keep total housing costs under 28% of your gross monthly income. Closing Map's mortgage calculator lets you model different purchase prices, down payment amounts, and rates side by side.

**Why get pre-approved for a mortgage first?**
Pre-approval tells sellers you're a serious buyer — not just browsing. It gives you a defined price range and speeds up the offer process when you find the right home. To get pre-approved, you'll share recent pay stubs, bank statements, and basic financial information with a lender.

**How does Closing Map help me find a good real estate agent?**

**What does a home inspection check?**
A licensed inspector looks at the structure, roof, foundation, electrical and plumbing systems, appliances, and overall condition of the property. You get a written report. If serious problems surface, you can ask the seller to repair them, credit you for the cost, or reduce the price — or you can walk away under your inspection contingency.

**What are contingencies in my offer?**
Contingencies are built-in exit conditions. Common ones include a financing contingency (you can exit if your mortgage falls through), an inspection contingency (you can renegotiate or exit if major issues are found), and an appraisal contingency (you're protected if the home appraises below your offer). They give you structured ways to back out safely if the deal goes wrong.

Sellers can still agree to cover your agent's fee — but that arrangement must be stated explicitly, not assumed. There are no hidden or automatic commissions. **What is due diligence after my offer is accepted?**
The due diligence period is typically 14 to 30 days. It's your window to inspect the home, verify the title, get the property appraised, and review all seller disclosures. If serious issues come up, your contingencies protect you. Think of it as a structured, time-limited investigation before you're fully committed.

**What papers do I sign at closing?**
The main documents are the Promissory Note (your promise to repay the loan), the Deed of Trust or Mortgage (the lender's security interest in the property), and the Closing Disclosure (a full breakdown of final costs). In New York, your real estate attorney reviews and explains each document with you at the closing table. You'll receive the Closing Disclosure at least three business days before closing — read it in advance.

**What should I do right after closing?**
Change the locks, transfer all utilities into your name, and store your closing documents securely. Update your address with USPS, your bank, your employer, and your insurance providers. If your home has a security system, change the credentials immediately.
