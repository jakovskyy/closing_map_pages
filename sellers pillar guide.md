## Step 1: Evaluate Your Home's Market Value

Before you list, you need a realistic sense of what your home is worth in today's market. The right valuation sets the tone for everything that follows — interest, speed, and your final net proceeds.

### Understanding Home Valuation

Your home's value is shaped by more than square footage or bedroom count. Several factors influence its market price:

- **Market conditions:** Is it a buyer's or seller's market? What does current inventory look like? Where are interest rates?
- **Location:** Proximity to schools, parks, shopping, and transit can boost value significantly.
- **Features and condition:** Upgrades, age of systems, recent renovations, and curb appeal all matter.
- **Comparable sales:** What have similar homes in your area recently sold for?
- **Buyer demand:** Hot neighborhoods and distinctive features attract more interest and competition.

Market value is what buyers will pay based on current conditions. Asking price is what you hope to receive. Setting your asking price too high leads to a slow sale. Too low and you leave money behind.

As of spring 2026, the NYC market is balanced with a slight seller's advantage in certain segments. Manhattan inventory sits at a five-year low, and well-priced condos in prime locations are moving in 30–45 days. Co-ops and mid-market properties are taking 90–120 days on average. Sellers who price correctly at entry are seeing competitive activity; overpriced listings are sitting. Your agent's read on current local conditions is essential before you commit to an asking price.

### Getting a Professional Appraisal

A licensed real estate appraiser gives you an unbiased, expert opinion on your home's value. An appraisal is especially useful if:

- You want a definitive value before listing
- Your property is unique or difficult to price
- You plan to negotiate confidently or need to satisfy lender requirements

There is a fee, but a professional appraisal can save you thousands by preventing mispricing early.

### Comparative Market Analysis (CMA)

Most real estate agents will provide a Comparative Market Analysis (CMA) — a report comparing your home to similar properties that have recently sold, are pending, or are currently listed. A CMA highlights:

- Listing and sale prices of comparable homes
- Price-per-square-foot trends
- Time on market statistics
- A recommended price range for your property

Request a CMA from your agent before you commit to a list price. A data-driven valuation is the foundation of everything else.

## Step 2: Prepare Your Home for Sale

Thoughtful preparation is one of the highest-leverage things you can do as a seller. The right updates, staging, and cleaning attract buyers and protect your asking price.

### Home Improvements and Repairs

Small upgrades and repairs can deliver significant returns. Focus on what buyers notice most:

- **Fresh paint:** Neutral colors brighten spaces and make rooms read as larger
- **Curb appeal:** Trim landscaping, mow the lawn, add seasonal flowers or mulch
- **Lighting:** Replace outdated fixtures and ensure all bulbs are working
- **Minor repairs:** Patch holes, fix leaky faucets, tighten loose cabinet handles, replace worn weatherstripping
- **Floors:** Clean carpets, polish hardwood, repair cracked tiles

Ask your agent for honest feedback. Even modest investment in repairs tends to pay back more than it costs.

### Staging Your Home

Staging helps buyers imagine themselves living in the space. It ranges from full furniture rental to simple rearranging:

- **Declutter:** Remove personal items, family photos, and excess furniture to open up rooms
- **Depersonalize:** Buyers should see a clean slate
- **Create inviting spaces:** Arrange furniture to highlight flow and purpose in each room
- **Add touches:** Fresh flowers, clean linens, and coordinated decor can sharpen a room's appeal

According to NAR's 2025 Profile of Home Staging, 49% of sellers' agents reported staged homes spent less time on market, and roughly 1 in 3 reported a 1%–10% increase in the price offered. On a $400,000 home, that's potentially $4,000–$40,000 in additional proceeds. Consider hiring a professional stager or ask your agent for guidance.

### Cleaning and Decluttering Checklist

A spotless home makes a powerful first impression. Before showings or photos, address these areas:

- Clean windows inside and out
- Vacuum carpets and mop floors
- Dust shelves, ceiling fans, and baseboards
- Scrub bathrooms and kitchens thoroughly
- Organize closets and cabinets — buyers will look
- Remove pet odors, litter boxes, or signs of pets
- Remove anything you don't plan to move — less is more

If time is short, consider a professional cleaning service.

### Ready for the Market

A well-prepared home speaks for itself. Buyers remember a space that feels fresh, well-maintained, and move-in ready.

- **Negotiation:** Your agent reviews offers, negotiates terms, and works to maximize your final number

A good agent helps you avoid costly mistakes and positions you for a clean, profitable sale.

## Step 4: Set the Right Price

Pricing your home correctly is one of the most consequential decisions in the process. A well-chosen price attracts buyers, generates offers, and protects your financial outcome. The wrong price — high or low — costs you.

### Pricing Strategies

- **Competitive pricing:** Set your asking price close to current market value, based on recent sales of comparable homes. This signals seriousness and invites offers.
- **Pricing slightly below market:** In a competitive market, some sellers price just below expected value to generate multiple offers and potentially push the final price higher.
- **Premium pricing:** If your home has meaningful upgrades or exceptional appeal, you may price above market — but overpricing limits interest and often leads to price cuts later.

Your agent can help you analyze local data and choose a strategy that balances speed, interest, and your bottom line.

### Understanding Buyer Psychology

- **First impressions matter:** Homes attract the most attention in the first two weeks after listing. An overpriced home causes buyers to hesitate or move on.
- **Price ranges:** Online search filters mean buyers set price brackets. Pricing within popular search ranges expands your pool of potential buyers.
- **Odd pricing:** Listing prices just below round numbers (e.g., $399,900 instead of $400,000) can read as more negotiable to buyers.
- **Anchoring:** Your asking price anchors negotiations. Too high, and buyers may not engage. Too low, and you risk accepting less than the property is worth.

### Adjusting the Price

Market feedback is a signal, not a judgment. If showings are frequent but offers are scarce, your price may be slightly high. If your home is sitting longer than comparable properties, consider adjusting. Work with your agent to review market data and buyer feedback regularly. A responsive pricing strategy keeps your listing competitive.

## Step 5: Market Your Home

Effective marketing turns a well-prepared, correctly priced home into a sold one. A strong marketing plan gets your property in front of the right buyers and generates the kind of interest that produces competitive offers.

### Marketing Strategies

A multi-channel approach is standard:

- **Online listings:** Most buyers start their search online. List on the MLS, Zillow, Realtor.com, and Redfin to maximize reach.
- **Social media:** Well-composed photos and virtual tours on Facebook and Instagram can draw attention. Local neighborhood groups are also worth targeting.
- **Open houses:** In-person or virtual open houses give buyers a chance to experience the space and ask questions directly.
- **Print and local marketing:** Yard signs, postcards, and flyers in local businesses can reach buyers who are already focused on your area.
- **Network outreach:** Your agent will market to their professional network, including agents who represent active buyers.

### Professional Photography

Most buyers form strong first impressions based on listing photos. Quality images are not optional.

- Hire a professional photographer to capture your home's strengths
- Consider video walkthroughs, drone exterior shots, and immersive 3D tours
- Bright, clean, well-composed images increase showing requests and support your asking price

### Listing Description Tips

Your listing description should do more than recite features. It should give buyers a reason to schedule a showing:

- Lead with your home's strongest attributes — light, space, upgrades, location
- Call out unique selling points: renovated kitchens, efficient systems, proximity to transit or schools
- Be clear about bedroom and bathroom count, square footage, and recent improvements
- Use specific, honest language — avoid vague superlatives and marketing clichés
- Plain language outperforms jargon

## Step 6: Show Your Home

Showings are where preparation and marketing pay off. This is the buyer's first real experience of your space. A welcoming, well-maintained showing can move a buyer from interest to offer.

### Preparing for Showings

- **Light:** Open curtains and blinds, turn on all lights. Bright spaces read as larger and more inviting.
- **Comfort:** Set a comfortable temperature.
- **Odor control:** Avoid strong scents. Air out the home and use neutral, mild air fresheners if needed.
- **Tidy up:** Make beds, clear counters, tidy bathrooms, empty trash cans before every showing.
- **Personal items:** Remove valuables and sensitive documents. Store family photos so buyers can picture their own lives there.
- **Pets:** Take pets with you or arrange for them to be elsewhere during showings.

### Open Houses: Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- Attracts buyers who might not schedule a private tour
- Generates buzz if well-publicized
- Allows flexible viewing times

**Cons:**
- Some visitors may be curious neighbors or unqualified buyers
- Security and privacy require basic precautions — secure valuables

Ask your agent whether an open house makes sense for your property type and market.

### Feedback from Showings

After each showing, your agent will gather feedback from buyers and their agents. Feedback reveals what buyers respond to, what is holding them back, and how your home compares to competing listings. Stay open to what you hear. Adjust your strategy — pricing, staging, or presentation — based on patterns in the feedback.

## Step 7: Review and Negotiate Offers

Receiving an offer is a milestone, not the finish line. Understanding how to evaluate and respond to offers protects your final outcome.

### Understanding Offers

When a buyer submits an offer, it will include:

- **Offered price:** The amount they are willing to pay
- **Earnest money deposit:** A good-faith deposit held in escrow
- **Contingencies:** Conditions that must be met before closing — commonly financing approval, appraisal, and inspection results
- **Proposed closing date:** When the buyer wants to take ownership
- **Requested inclusions:** Appliances or fixtures the buyer wants included

Read every term carefully. Your agent can break down the details so you understand what you are agreeing to.

### Negotiation Tips

- **Respond promptly:** Serious buyers appreciate timely counteroffers.
- **Look beyond price:** Contingencies, closing timeline, and earnest money all affect the quality of a deal. A slightly lower price with fewer contingencies and a faster close can be the better outcome.
- **Know when to hold:** If your home is competitively priced and generating interest, you are not obligated to accept the first offer.
- **Evaluate buyer strength:** Pre-approval, a large earnest deposit, and a flexible closing date are indicators of a reliable buyer.

Your agent is your primary negotiator. Use their experience.

### Counteroffers

You are not required to accept an offer as written. Your options are:

- **Accept:** If the terms work, sign and move forward
- **Reject:** If price or terms are unacceptable, decline
- **Counter:** Adjust the price, conditions, closing date, or inclusions and return it to the buyer

Counteroffers are routine. The process may go back and forth before both sides reach agreement.

## Step 8: Home Inspection and Appraisal

After you accept an offer, the buyer will typically order an inspection and, if they are financing, an appraisal. These steps protect all parties but can introduce new negotiating points.

### Preparing for Inspections

A licensed inspector will assess the home's structure, systems, and major components. The buyer typically pays for the inspection. Expect the inspector to evaluate:

- Roof, foundation, and exterior
- Electrical, plumbing, and HVAC systems
- Attic, basement, and crawl spaces
- Appliances, windows, doors, and insulation

To prepare:

- Ensure all utilities are on
- Provide clear access to the attic, basement, water heater, and electrical panel
- Address minor issues (leaky faucets, burned-out bulbs) in advance
- Leave the home for the duration of the inspection if possible

### New York Property Condition Disclosure Statement

New York sellers of 1–4 family homes are required to complete a Property Condition Disclosure Statement (PCDS) before closing — a 56-question form covering structural conditions, mechanical systems, environmental hazards, and a dedicated flood-risk section. The former option to skip the form in exchange for a $500 credit was eliminated in March 2024. Work with your attorney to complete it accurately and early. Filing it late or inaccurately creates legal exposure.

### Addressing Issues

After the inspection, buyers may request repairs or a price reduction based on the findings.

- **Negotiate repairs:** You can agree to fix items or offer a closing credit in lieu of repairs. In a strong market, you can decline minor requests.
- **Keep documentation:** If you complete repairs, retain receipts showing that licensed professionals performed the work.

Work with your agent to prioritize what to address. Major issues — roof, foundation, safety hazards — are generally worth resolving to keep the deal intact.

### The Appraisal Process

If the buyer is getting a mortgage, the lender will order an appraisal to confirm the property's value supports the contract price.

- **If it appraises at or above the sale price:** No issue — proceed to closing
- **If it appraises below the sale price:** The buyer may request a price adjustment, agree to cover the gap in cash, or cancel if the contract allows

Sellers can support the appraisal by providing documentation of upgrades and recent comparable sales to the appraiser. If a gap exists, be open to negotiation.

## Step 9: Closing the Sale

Once inspections and appraisals are complete and all conditions are met, you are ready for closing. This is the final step before handing over the keys.

### Understanding Closing

Closing — also called settlement — is the meeting where ownership officially transfers. In New York, both the buyer and the seller are required to have their own real estate attorney present at closing. Your attorney, not your agent, handles the legal review of documents and ensures your interests are protected. This is not optional and is not a formality. Engage your attorney early in the process, ideally before you accept an offer.

At closing, you will:

- Review and sign required documents
- Pay any remaining fees or outstanding liens from proceeds
- Hand over keys, garage remotes, and security codes to the buyer
- Confirm that all agreed repairs and inclusions are complete

### New York Transfer Taxes

In New York City, sellers pay a combined 1.4%–2.075% in city and state transfer taxes, depending on sale price. The breakdown: 1% city + 0.4% state on sales under $500,000; 1.425% city + 0.4% state on sales over $500,000. The mansion tax — 1%–3.9% on purchases of $1 million or more — is a buyer cost, not a seller cost, though it can affect negotiations near that threshold. Factor these into your net proceeds estimate before closing day. Run the net sheet before you finalize your bottom line.

### Essential Documents Checklist for Sellers at Closing

- **Closing Disclosure (CD):** Details all financial transactions — your final payoff amounts, closing costs, agent commissions, and net proceeds. Review for accuracy at least three days before closing.
- **Deed Transfer:** The legal document transferring ownership from you to the buyer.
- **Bill of Sale:** Transfers ownership of any specific items included in the sale, such as appliances or fixtures.
- **Affidavit of Title (or Seller's Affidavit):** Confirms you are the rightful owner and that there are no undisclosed liens, assessments, or legal issues affecting the property.
- **Payoff Statements:** From your mortgage lender or any lien holder, showing exact amounts needed to pay off loans at closing.
- **IRS Form 1099-S:** Reports the property sale to the IRS for tax purposes.
- **Settlement Statement (ALTA or HUD-1):** Itemizes all costs and credits in the transaction.
- **Certificate for No Tax Withholding (if applicable):** Used if you are exempt from certain withholding requirements.
- **Additional documents:** Local forms, HOA paperwork, warranties, or repair receipts as required by your state, city, or sales contract.

### Key Roles at Closing

- **Closing Agent / Escrow Company:** Neutral third party that handles funds, reviews documents, and ensures all contract conditions are met
- **Title Company:** Verifies clear ownership and issues title insurance for the buyer and lender
- **Attorneys:** In New York, both buyer and seller are required to have their own real estate attorney at closing. Your attorney reviews all legal documents, advises on your obligations, and ensures the transaction complies with New York law.
- **Lenders:** Provide loan funds if the buyer is financing and confirm final payoff details

### What to Bring to Closing

- Government-issued photo ID
- House keys, mailbox keys, garage remotes, and access codes
- Proof of any agreed repairs or warranties
- Bank account information if receiving proceeds via wire transfer

### The Final Walkthrough

Within 24–48 hours before closing, the buyer will do a final walkthrough to confirm the property is in the agreed condition, all negotiated repairs are complete, and no new damage has occurred. Address any issues identified at the walkthrough immediately. Unresolved items can delay or derail closing.

## Step 10: After the Sale — Your Post-Closing Checklist

You have sold your home. A few important steps remain before you close this chapter.

### Moving Out

- **Move out by the agreed date:** Confirm the possession date in your contract. If you arranged a rent-back, honor those terms precisely.
- **Clean the property:** Leave the home broom-swept and free of debris.
- **Remove all personal items:** Check the attic, basement, garage, and any outbuildings.
- **Return all keys and codes:** Hand over house keys, garage remotes, mailbox keys, and security codes.

### Wrap Up Utilities and Mail

- Contact all utility providers — gas, electric, water, trash, internet, cable — to schedule final readings and shutoff dates.
- File an address change with USPS. Update your address with banks, insurers, credit cards, and subscriptions.

### Secure Your Sale Paperwork

Keep copies of your closing disclosure, settlement statement, deed transfer, and proof of proceeds in a secure location. You will need these for tax filings and future reference.

### Capital Gains and Taxes

Federal law lets single sellers exclude up to $250,000 in gain from a primary residence sale ($500,000 for married couples filing jointly), provided you have owned and lived in the home for at least two of the last five years. Gain above those thresholds is taxable — and New York sellers face a meaningful state tax bill on top. New York taxes capital gains as ordinary income. Combined federal, state, and city rates can exceed 40% on non-excluded gain for NYC residents. Document every capital improvement you have made; each dollar added to your cost basis reduces your taxable gain. Consult a tax advisor before closing, not after.

### Address Outstanding Items

- **Final repairs or agreements:** If you agreed to complete any work after closing, or must transfer warranties or service contracts, handle these promptly.
- **HOA requirements:** Notify your homeowners' association of the sale and pay any required transfer fees.

## FAQ

**What does it cost to sell my home in New York?**
Selling in New York typically costs 8–10% of the sale price when you account for agent fees, transfer taxes, attorney fees, repairs, staging, and other closing costs. **How do I know what my home is worth?**
Look at what comparable homes nearby have sold for recently — this is a Comparative Market Analysis (CMA). You can also get a professional appraisal for an independent, unbiased opinion. Your agent will walk you through the data so you price accurately from the start.

**What easy fixes help sell my home faster?**
Fresh neutral paint, clean carpets, fixed leaks, and a tidy yard make a meaningful difference. Declutter and stage rooms so buyers can picture themselves in the space. These low-cost changes often protect or increase your sale price.

**How should I price my home to sell?**
Price close to what comparable homes have sold for to attract buyers quickly. Pricing slightly under market in a competitive environment can spark multiple offers. Overpricing leads to longer days on market and eventual price cuts. Work with your agent to read local conditions and adjust based on feedback.

**What is the best way to market my home?**
Professional photography, MLS listing, open houses, and targeted social media posts are the core. Your listing description should highlight real features — updates, location, light — not generic language. Your agent handles execution; your job is to have the home ready.

**How do I evaluate and respond to buyer offers?**
Review the offered price, closing date, contingencies, and earnest money deposit. If the terms are not right, counter. Your agent guides the negotiation. The goal is the deal that maximizes your net proceeds — not necessarily the highest headline number.

Major issues are generally worth addressing to protect the deal. Work with your agent to decide what to accept or negotiate.

You will sign the deed transferring ownership, payoff documents for your mortgage, and various affidavits about the property's condition and title.
